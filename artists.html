Artists

The artists featured at MoMA represent some of the most influential and groundbreaking voices in modern and contemporary art. From pioneers of abstraction and surrealism to innovators in pop culture and conceptual art, these artists have challenged conventions, expanded artistic boundaries, and reshaped how we see the world.

1880s - 1940s

Vincent Van Gogh

Dutch, 1853 – 1890

Vincent van Gogh was a pivotal figure in the transition from 19th-century Realism to modernist abstraction. His expressive use of color, dynamic brushwork, and emotionally charged compositions—seen in works like The Starry Night and Sunflowers—laid the groundwork for movements such as Fauvism and Expressionism, making him a cornerstone of modern art history.

Pablo Picasso

Spanish, 1881 – 1973

Pablo Picasso was a foundational figure in 20th-century art, known for his radical innovations that shaped the course of modernism. From pioneering Cubism with Les Demoiselles d’Avignon to his emotive political work Guernica, Picasso’s vast and varied body of work challenged traditional representation and redefined the possibilities of artistic expression.

Frida Kahlo

Mexican, 1907 – 1954

Frida Kahlo was a central figure in modern art whose deeply personal and symbolic paintings bridged Surrealism, Mexican folk traditions, and political commentary. Through works like The Two Fridas and Self-Portrait with Cropped Hair, she explored themes of identity, pain, and resilience, leaving a lasting impact on both art history and cultural discourse.

René Magritte

Belgian, 1898 – 1967

René Magritte was a leading figure of Surrealism, renowned for his witty and thought-provoking images that challenged perceptions of reality. Through iconic works like The Treachery of Images and The Son of Man, Magritte used ordinary objects in unexpected contexts, inviting viewers to question the relationship between language, representation, and meaning.

1950s - 1970s

Mark Rothko

American, 1903 – 1970

Mark Rothko was a key figure in Abstract Expressionism, known for his luminous fields of color that evoke deep emotional and spiritual experiences. Through works like No. 10 and Orange and Yellow, Rothko sought to create spaces for contemplation, using color and scale to communicate the profound depths of human feeling.

Andy Warhol

American, 1928 – 1987

Andy Warhol was a leading figure of Pop Art, celebrated for transforming everyday consumer goods and celebrity icons into bold, iconic artworks. Through pieces like Gold Marilyn Monroe and Campbell’s Soup Cans, Warhol challenged traditional boundaries between high art and popular culture, redefining the relationship between art, media, and society.

Jackson Pollock

American, 1912 – 1956

Jackson Pollock was a pioneering force in Abstract Expressionism, known for his revolutionary drip painting technique that emphasized movement, spontaneity, and the physical act of creation. In works like One: Number 31, 1950, Pollock redefined the canvas as a space of dynamic energy, breaking away from traditional composition and radically expanding the possibilities of modern art.

Roy Lichtenstein

American, 1923 – 1997

Roy Lichtenstein was a major figure in the Pop Art movement, renowned for his bold, comic book–inspired paintings that elevated mass media imagery into high art. Through works like Drowning Girl and Whaam!, Lichtenstein used Ben-Day dots, sharp lines, and ironic humor to explore themes of consumerism, popular culture, and the nature of artistic reproduction.

1980s - Present

Keith Haring

American, 1958 – 1990

Keith Haring was a prominent artist whose bold, graphic style transformed street culture into a powerful form of visual art. Through vibrant works like his untitled subway drawings and large-scale murals, Haring addressed themes of community, activism, and social justice with an immediacy and energy that made his art accessible to a wide audience.

David Wojnarowicz

American, 1954 – 1992

David Wojnarowicz was a fiercely expressive artist whose work blended photography, painting, writing, and performance to confront issues of identity, marginalization, and the AIDS crisis. Through powerful pieces like Untitled (One Day This Kid...), he used art as a form of political activism, challenging societal norms and giving voice to those silenced by prejudice and injustice.

Felix Gonzalez-Torres

American, born Cuba. 1957 – 1996

Felix Gonzalez-Torres was a conceptual artist known for his poignant, minimalist installations that invited viewer participation and explored themes of love, loss, and memory. Through works like "Untitled" (Perfect Lovers) and his candy spill pieces, Gonzalez-Torres blurred the boundaries between public and private experience, creating deeply personal yet universally resonant art.

Richard Serra

American, 1938 – 2024

Richard Serra is a pioneering sculptor known for his monumental steel installations that transform space and challenge perceptions of balance, weight, and movement. Through works like Torqued Ellipse IV, Serra invites viewers to physically engage with his sculptures, experiencing art as an immersive and dynamic encounter.
